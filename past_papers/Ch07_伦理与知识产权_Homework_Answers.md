## Question 1 [4 marks]

**(a)** Explain what is meant by **copyright** in relation to software. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ Copyright is the legal right automatically granted to the creator/author of a piece of software, giving them exclusive control over how the software is used, copied, distributed, and modified
✓ It means that no one else may legally copy, distribute, or modify the software without the permission of the copyright holder; violation of copyright (software piracy) is a criminal offence

**(b)** Describe **two** ways a software developer can protect their software from being illegally copied. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ **Software licensing** — the developer can require users to accept a licence agreement and enter a unique product/licence key before the software can be installed or activated; this limits usage to authorised purchasers
✓ **Digital Rights Management (DRM) / copy protection** — the developer can implement technical measures such as requiring online activation, limiting the number of devices the software can be installed on, or using code obfuscation to make it difficult to reverse-engineer or crack the software

## Question 3 [6 marks]

**(a)** State **three** principles commonly included in data protection laws. [3 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ Personal data must be collected and processed fairly and lawfully — data should only be collected with the individual's knowledge/consent and for a specified, legitimate purpose
✓ Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date — organisations must take reasonable steps to ensure data is correct and allow individuals to request corrections
✓ Personal data must not be kept for longer than necessary — data should be securely deleted/destroyed once it is no longer needed for the purpose for which it was collected

**(b)** Explain why data protection laws are important for individuals. [3 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ They give individuals the right to know what personal data is held about them and how it is being used, providing transparency
✓ They protect individuals from having their personal information misused, sold to third parties without consent, or used for purposes they did not agree to — preventing identity theft and fraud
✓ They give individuals the right to request that incorrect data be corrected and that their data be deleted, giving them control over their own personal information

## Question 4 [6 marks]

**(a)** Describe **two** ethical concerns related to the use of AI in decision-making. [4 marks]

**Answer:**

1. ✓ **Bias and discrimination** — AI systems are trained on historical data which may contain biases; if the training data reflects existing prejudices (e.g. gender, racial), the AI may make discriminatory decisions
   ✓ For example, an AI recruitment tool trained on historical hiring data may unfairly reject candidates from underrepresented groups

2. ✓ **Lack of transparency / explainability** — many AI systems (especially deep learning models) operate as "black boxes" where the reasoning behind decisions is not easily understood or explained
   ✓ This makes it difficult for individuals to challenge decisions that affect them (e.g. a loan rejection) and raises concerns about accountability — if an AI makes a wrong decision, it is unclear who is responsible

**(b)** Suggest **one** measure to address the ethical concerns. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ Organisations could implement mandatory bias auditing — regularly testing and evaluating AI systems using diverse datasets to identify and correct biases before and after deployment
✓ This ensures that the AI system is fair and does not discriminate against any group; results of audits could be published to increase transparency and accountability

## Question 5 [4 marks]

**(a)** Explain what is meant by the **digital divide**. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ The digital divide is the gap/inequality between groups of people who have access to modern information and communication technology (e.g. computers, internet) and those who do not
✓ This divide can exist between countries (e.g. developed vs. developing nations), within a country (e.g. urban vs. rural areas), or between different demographic groups (e.g. based on age, income, education)

**(b)** Describe **two** consequences of the digital divide. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ **Educational disadvantage** — individuals without access to technology and the internet cannot access online learning resources, digital textbooks, or e-learning platforms, putting them at a disadvantage compared to their peers who have access
✓ **Economic disadvantage** — individuals without digital access may miss out on job opportunities that require digital skills or are advertised online; they may also be unable to access online banking, e-commerce, and government services, limiting their economic participation

## Question 6 [6 marks]

**(a)** Describe **two** negative environmental impacts of computing technology. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ **Energy consumption** — data centres, servers, and the billions of computing devices in use worldwide consume vast amounts of electricity, much of which is generated from fossil fuels, contributing to carbon emissions and climate change
✓ **Resource depletion** — the manufacture of computing devices requires rare earth metals and other finite natural resources; mining these materials causes environmental damage including habitat destruction and water pollution

**(b)** Describe **two** ways computing technology can **reduce** environmental damage. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ **Remote working and video conferencing** — technology enables people to work from home and hold virtual meetings, reducing the need for commuting and business travel, which lowers carbon emissions from transport
✓ **Smart systems / environmental monitoring** — sensors and computer-controlled systems can optimise energy use in buildings (smart thermostats, lighting), manage traffic flow to reduce congestion, and monitor environmental conditions (air quality, deforestation) to support conservation efforts

**(c)** Explain what is meant by **e-waste** and why it is a growing concern. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ E-waste (electronic waste) refers to discarded electronic devices and components (e.g. old computers, smartphones, monitors, circuit boards) that are no longer in use
✓ It is a growing concern because the rapid pace of technological change means devices are replaced frequently, leading to increasing volumes of waste; many electronic components contain hazardous materials (e.g. lead, mercury, cadmium) that can leak into soil and water if not disposed of properly, causing environmental pollution and health risks

## Question 7 [4 marks]

Describe **four** activities that would be offences under a Computer Misuse Act.

**Answer:**

✓ **Unauthorised access to computer material** — intentionally accessing a computer system or data without permission (e.g. hacking into someone's account), even if no data is modified or stolen
✓ **Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences** — gaining unauthorised access to a system with the intention of committing another crime, such as fraud or blackmail (e.g. accessing bank records to steal money)
✓ **Unauthorised modification of computer material** — intentionally altering, deleting, or corrupting data or programs without permission (e.g. planting a virus, deleting files, changing database records)
✓ **Making, supplying, or obtaining tools for computer misuse** — creating, distributing, or possessing software or tools designed to facilitate computer crimes (e.g. writing and distributing hacking tools or malware)

## Question 8 [6 marks]

**(a)** Identify **two** privacy concerns raised by this scenario. [2 marks]

**Answer:**

✓ The company is sharing users' personal data (location, browsing history, private messages) with third-party companies without the users' explicit knowledge or informed consent
✓ The company is collecting private messages, which users would reasonably expect to be confidential; using this data for advertising purposes is an invasion of privacy

**(b)** Discuss the ethical issues involved. [4 marks]

**Company's perspective:**
✓ The company may argue that targeted advertising generates the revenue needed to provide the social media service for free to users, and that users agree to data collection through the terms of service
✓ The company may claim that anonymised/aggregated data sharing benefits users through more relevant advertisements and an improved user experience

**Users' perspective:**
✓ Users have a right to privacy and should have clear, transparent information about what data is being collected and how it is used; burying data-sharing practices in lengthy terms of service is not genuine informed consent
✓ Users may not be aware that their private messages and browsing history are being analysed and shared; this represents a betrayal of trust and could expose them to risks such as identity theft, manipulation, or discrimination if the data is mishandled by third parties

## Question 9 [6 marks]

**Open source software:**

✓ **Advantage — Cost:** Open source software is typically free to download and use, which is beneficial for a school with a limited IT budget; there are no licence fees to pay per machine
✓ **Advantage — Customisation:** The source code is available, so the school's IT staff (or community) can modify and adapt the software to meet the school's specific needs
✓ **Disadvantage — Support:** There is usually no dedicated customer support team; the school must rely on community forums and documentation, which may not provide timely or reliable help when issues arise
✓ **Disadvantage — Security:** While many people can review the code, vulnerabilities may still exist and may be exploited before patches are released; the school must actively monitor and apply updates

**Proprietary software:**

✓ **Advantage — Support:** Proprietary software typically comes with dedicated professional technical support, training materials, and regular updates from the vendor, which is valuable for a school without specialist IT staff
✓ **Advantage — Security:** The vendor is responsible for identifying and patching security vulnerabilities and releasing updates; schools receive regular, managed security patches
✓ **Disadvantage — Cost:** Proprietary software requires purchasing licences for each machine or user, which can be very expensive for a school, especially when licences must be renewed annually
✓ **Disadvantage — Customisation:** The source code is not available, so the school cannot modify the software to suit its specific requirements; it must use the software as provided by the vendor
